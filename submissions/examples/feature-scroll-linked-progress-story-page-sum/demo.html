The Quiet Craft of Motion on the Web

A long-form story about scroll-driven storytelling, progressive disclosure, and respecting readers who prefer less animation.

Five chapters · ~8 min read

Chapter 1 — Why scroll matters

Scroll is the oldest interaction on the web. Long before touch gestures and keyboard shortcuts, readers moved through documents by dragging a viewport down a page. That simple gesture still carries narrative weight: each pixel of movement is a beat in the story.

When designers link motion to scroll position, they create a shared rhythm between content and reader. A progress bar at the top answers a small but meaningful question: how much remains?

Footnote: Fixed progress indicators should expose role="progressbar" and update aria-valuenow for screen-reader users.

Chapter 2 — Chapters that arrive

Instead of dumping an entire article at once, chapter sections can fade in as they enter the viewport. Intersection Observer makes this efficient: the browser tells you when an element crosses a threshold, and you toggle a class.

“Motion should reveal structure, not decorate emptiness.”

The effect is subtle — a short upward drift paired with opacity — but it guides the eye and signals that a new section has begun. Readers who skim still benefit from clear headings; those who read linearly get a gentle sense of progression.

Footnote: Keep initial opacity at zero only when motion is allowed. Under reduced motion, show all content immediately.

Chapter 3 — Footnotes that pop in

Supplementary notes deserve their own moment. Rather than cluttering the main column, footnotes can wait until they are near the reader’s focus, then slide in from the margin with a slight scale correction.

This pattern works well for citations, definitions, and accessibility callouts. The main narrative stays clean; curious readers discover depth on their own timeline.

Footnote: Use a lower intersection threshold for footnotes so they appear slightly before the reader finishes the preceding paragraph.

Chapter 4 — Performance and politeness

Scroll listeners that run on every frame can jank on low-end devices. Reading scrollHeight and scrollTop inside a passive listener is usually fine for a thin progress bar, but avoid heavy layout work during scroll.

Intersection Observer callbacks batch visibility changes and keep main-thread work predictable. Pair observers with CSS transitions so JavaScript only toggles classes.

Footnote: Disconnect observers after one-time reveals if footnotes should not re-animate when scrolling back up.

Chapter 5 — Reduced motion as a first-class path

Not every reader wants animation. The prefers-reduced-motion media query is a contract: when it matches, skip entrance effects and show content in its final state. Progress bars may remain — they convey information, not decoration.

Building two paths from the start is easier than bolting accessibility on later. CSS handles the static fallback; JavaScript checks the same preference before adding animated classes.

Footnote: Test with reduced motion enabled in your OS accessibility settings to verify chapters and footnotes appear immediately.
